# BhuMe Take-Home: Implementation Strategy

**Goal:** Platinum tier — one method, zero per-village hand-tuning, that corrects plot boundaries across both villages, knows when it's wrong, and proves it. Optimize for the three things they explicitly grade: accuracy improvement (IoU > 0.5 over official position), **confidence calibration (AUC — their highest-weighted signal)**, and restraint (not moving already-correct plots).

**Core thesis the whole method hangs on:** The official boundaries didn't drift randomly. Paper cadastral sheets were georeferenced with sparse control points (bunds, roads, tanks), so error is a **smooth, spatially coherent drift field** — neighboring plots drift together, and drift varies gradually across the village. A method that estimates this *field* rather than matching each plot greedily is (a) more accurate where image signal is weak, (b) gives a principled uncertainty for confidence, and (c) mirrors the actual physical cause of the error — which makes for a defensible story, not just a score.

A second independent signal: the `drawn ÷ recorded` area ratio. Near 1.0 → the shape is right, only placement is wrong (fixable by registration). Far from 1.0 → the record and the drawing disagree at a deeper level (stale outline, split parcels, digitisation error) — no amount of shifting fixes that, so **flag, don't drag**.

---

## Phase 0 — Data forensics (before writing any method code)

Deliberately slow start. Every threshold downstream comes from here, not from guessing.

1. Load both bundles. Render official plots over imagery; build a quick clickable HTML map (folium/leaflet) for visual inspection — this artifact stays useful through the whole project and goes in the repo.
2. **Quantify the drift hypothesis:** use the 9 example truths (6 Vadnerbhairav + 3 Malatavadi): compute true shift vectors (truth centroid − official centroid). Check magnitude distribution and whether nearby truths share direction. This validates or kills the drift-field thesis on day one.
3. **Area-ratio census:** histogram drawn/recorded ratio across all ~5k plots per village. Establishes what fraction of plots are placement-fixable vs record-broken, and sets the flag threshold empirically.
4. **Signal audit:** sample ~30 plots per regime (open field / tree cover / village core / tiny parcel). For each, inspect imagery gradient and boundaries.tif. Output: an honest map of where each signal can be trusted. Malatavadi's 872 m² median plot (~30×30 m, a handful of pixels of edge) is the stress case — confirm early how much signal even exists there.
5. **boundaries.tif characterization:** precision/recall of the hint layer against visible bunds in sampled patches. It's labeled ROUGH by the organisers — measure *how* rough before deciding its fusion weight.

## Phase 1 — Baseline ladder

Score each rung with the kit's `score()` on example truths, keep a results table from day one:

1. **Identity** (don't move anything) — the restraint reference.
2. **`global_median_shift()`** (provided) — the naive reference to beat.
3. **Greedy per-plot match** (Phase 2 alone, no field) — proves the drift field adds value later, becomes an ablation row.

This ladder is itself evidence of method discipline — it shows in the video that every component earned its place.

## Phase 2 — Boundary evidence map (signal engineering)

Build one fused **"boundary likelihood" raster** per village from independent channels:

- Image gradients (Sobel magnitude on luminance) — crisp bunds, roads.
- Vegetation transitions — band ratios (pseudo-NDVI from RGB if NIR absent); field boundaries are often crop-state changes, not visible ridges.
- Texture boundaries — local variance shifts catch ploughed-vs-fallow edges gradients miss.
- `boundaries.tif` hints, weighted by the Phase 0 reliability audit (down-weighted near buildings/canopy).

Fuse to a single likelihood map, then compute its **distance transform** once per village → cheap chamfer matching everywhere. Important restraint on my own engineering: cap time here. A decent fused map plus a strong matching/field/calibration stack beats a perfect edge map with a greedy matcher.

## Phase 3 — Per-plot registration

For each candidate plot (area-ratio sane):

1. Crop patch (`patch_for_plot()`), rasterize the official outline.
2. **Chamfer matching:** slide the outline over a translation grid (search radius set from Phase 0 drift stats, scaled by patch resolution), score = robust mean of edge-distance along the outline (trimmed mean, so one occluded edge doesn't sink the match). Coarse-to-fine for speed.
3. Optionally refine with small rotation (±2–3°) — paper sheets warp slightly.
4. Keep the full **score landscape**, not just the argmax: peak sharpness and the ratio of best to second-best peak are the raw material for confidence. A flat or multi-peaked landscape is the method telling us it doesn't know — we listen later instead of discarding this.
5. **Block matching variant:** match a plot *jointly with its immediate neighbors* as a rigid group. Since drift is sheet-level, a 5-plot block has far more edge to lock onto than one tiny parcel — this is what rescues Malatavadi's 30×30 m plots. Compare block vs solo match per plot; agreement is another confidence signal.

## Phase 4 — Drift field (the centerpiece)

1. Select **anchors**: plots with sharp, unambiguous solo matches (top quantile of landscape sharpness, spatially spread).
2. Fit a smooth 2-D vector field over anchor shifts — **Gaussian process regression** (or RBF interpolation with residual bootstrapping as the simpler fallback). The GP is deliberately chosen because it returns **predictive variance**: the field says both "expected drift here" and "how sure I am," and that variance flows straight into the confidence model.
3. **Robustness loop:** discard anchors whose shift is an outlier vs the local field (bad match that looked sharp), refit. 1–2 iterations.
4. **Second matching pass, field-regularized:** redo per-plot matching with the field prior as a penalty — final shift = argmax(edge evidence + λ·field prior). Weak-signal plots lean on the field; strong-signal plots can override it locally. This EM-flavoured alternation (match → field → rematch) is run a fixed small number of rounds.
5. Sanity guard: after shifting, **neighbor overlap check** — corrected plots shouldn't newly overlap each other or detach from the parcel fabric. Violations cost confidence or trigger a flag.

## Phase 5 — Decision layer (corrected / flagged / leave alone)

Explicit, auditable rules — each plot's `method_note` states which path it took:

- **Leave alone (not attempted / restraint):** best shift below a minimum-meaningful threshold (sub-pixel / sub-meter), or shifted IoU gain over official is negligible. Protects against the control plots they seeded.
- **Flag:** area ratio outside sane band; flat/multi-peak landscape *and* high field variance; solo and block matches disagree; post-shift overlap violations. Flagged plots keep original geometry plus a note saying *why* — they read the logic behind flags.
- **Correct:** everything else, geometry = official outline translated (+ small rotation if refined).

## Phase 6 — Confidence calibration (highest-weighted, gets its own phase)

The trap: only 9 ground-truth plots — tuning to them is overfitting they explicitly penalize. So manufacture a large validation set instead:

1. **Synthetic ground truth:** take plots where the method is most certain (sharp landscape + field agreement + the 9 truths), treat their corrected position as truth, inject *known* random shifts (drawn from the Phase 0 drift distribution), re-run the pipeline blind, and measure recovery (does it find the injected truth? final IoU?). Hundreds of (match-metrics → actual outcome) pairs, free, per regime.
2. **Calibration model:** map raw signals — landscape sharpness, peak ratio, solo/block agreement, GP field variance, area-ratio sanity — to P(IoU > 0.5) via **isotonic regression** (monotone, can't overfit wiggles) fit on the synthetic set. That probability *is* the submitted confidence — it means something by construction.
3. **Evidence artifacts:** reliability diagram (predicted confidence vs realized accuracy on synthetic set) and AUC on synthetic + the 9 truths. These go in the repo and the video — direct proof of the skill they say they watch most.

## Phase 7 — Generalization proof (Platinum)

- One pipeline, one config. All adaptive parameters derived from the data itself (search radius from drift census, patch scale from median plot size, fusion weights from the signal audit) — *automatic adaptation, zero hand-tuning per village*, exactly the Platinum wording.
- Run both villages end-to-end fresh. Expect lower mean confidence on Malatavadi — that's the calibration working, and gets said out loud in the video rather than hidden.
- **Ablation table:** field off, block-matching off, boundaries.tif off, calibration replaced by raw score → metric deltas. Quantifies what each idea buys.
- **Failure gallery:** 5–10 curated plots where the method flagged or failed, each with one sentence on why. Demonstrating honest self-knowledge of the method is itself rubric currency here.

## Phase 8 — Deliverables

1. **Repo:** `predict.py <village_dir>` → contract-shaped `predictions.geojson`; clean module layout (`signal.py`, `matching.py`, `drift_field.py`, `calibrate.py`, `decide.py`); README with a method diagram, the baseline-ladder table, ablations, reliability diagram, failure gallery; the interactive before/after HTML map as a bonus reviewers can click.

## Revised Phase 3 — Dynamic Block Matching

**Adjacency graph, built once per village.** Nodes = plots. Edges = shared-boundary contiguity (polygon touch with snap tolerance), backstopped by centroid Delaunay with a distance cap to catch road/stream-separated neighbors. This graph is reused everywhere downstream: block growing, fabric checks, seam detection in Phase 4.

**Block growing — evidence-budget rule, no hardcoded block size.** Each plot needs a minimum amount of *matchable edge* to register reliably. Define a per-plot evidence budget:

> budget(plot) = Σ over its boundary segments of (segment length × signal reliability of the regime that segment sits in)

— reliability per regime (open field / canopy / village core) comes from the Phase 0 signal audit. Then:

1. Start with the target plot. If budget ≥ threshold → solo match. Open-field Vadnerbhairav plots mostly stop here.
2. Else grow the block: add adjacent plots in order of budget contributed per area added, until the *block's* budget clears threshold or a radius cap is hit. Malatavadi's 30×30 m parcels naturally grow 5–15-plot blocks; the block size *emerges from local signal poverty* rather than being a parameter.
3. Constraint: blocks never cross a detected sheet seam (Phase 4) — a block spanning two independently georeferenced sheets would be matching a rigid template against genuinely incoherent drift.

**Matching weights inside a block.** The block's outer envelope edges get full weight; interior edges get weight proportional to their measured visibility (interior bunds between same-crop fields are often invisible — counting them as evidence inflates false sharpness). Chamfer match the weighted block outline as one rigid template.

**Solo/block disagreement remains a confidence signal**, now sharpened: solo match exists only where solo budget was sufficient, so disagreement means two *adequately-powered* estimates conflict — strong flag/down-weight trigger, not noise.

## Revised Phase 4 — Multi-Sheet Discontinuities & Topology

### Sheet seam detection (Phase 0/4)

Three detectors, cheapest first, agreeing detectors raise confidence in a seam:

1. **Metadata blockiness (Phase 0, free).** Cadastral sheets were drawn over survey-number blocks. Check whether plot_number / survey-number ranges form spatially contiguous patches; patch borders are sheet-boundary *candidates*. Also: sheet edges historically follow physical features — roads, streams — which we can see in imagery.
2. **Anchor-shift discordance on the adjacency graph (primary detector).** For each graph edge between two anchors, compute shift discordance ‖shift_i − shift_j‖. Intra-sheet discordance is small and smooth; across a seam it's large and — critically — **spatially coherent along a line**. Delete edges above a threshold (set from the intra-sheet discordance distribution, not hand-picked), take connected components. Isolated bad anchors produce salt-and-pepper deletions (absorbed by the RANSAC consensus filter); true seams produce a *connected cut* separating two sizeable components.
3. **Model-selection guard against over-segmentation.** A split is only accepted if the piecewise model (per-component field) beats the single smooth field on **leave-one-out anchor residual** by a meaningful margin. This is the brake: torn-cloth warping *within* a sheet should be absorbed by the smooth field, not spawn fake seams.

### Per-sheet model — hierarchical, and this also answers the affine-warp critique

Within each detected sheet component:

> **sheet-level affine (6 DOF — captures the systematic stretch/skew of that sheet's georeferencing) + GP on the residuals (captures smooth local cloth-warp).**

The affine soaks up exactly the "torn cloth" stretch the critique raised; the GP only has to model what's left, which keeps it well-behaved and keeps its predictive variance meaningful. Plots near a seam inherit extra variance (distance-to-seam term added to GP variance) → lower confidence → more flags exactly where two sheets disagree about reality. That's calibration behaving correctly, and it's a curated example for the failure gallery.

### Topology — the key design decision

**Transform vertices through the field, not polygons through a translation.** This dissolves the tearing problem rather than patching it:

- The final correction is a continuous map T(x, y) (sheet affine ∘ smooth residual field). Apply T to every *vertex*.
- Adjacent plots that share boundary vertices (verify vertex-identity in input.geojson during Phase 0; snap if near-identical) map those vertices **identically by construction** — the fabric stretches, never tears, with no explicit topology data structure, no shared-vertex bookkeeping, no least-squares parcel adjustment. Continuity of T *is* the topology guarantee.
